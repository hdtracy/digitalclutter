A **philosophical zombie** (or "**p-zombie**") is a being in a [thought experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought_experiment "Thought experiment") in the [philosophy of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind "Philosophy of mind") that is physically identical to a normal human being but does not have [conscious experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consciousness "Consciousness").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_zombie#cite_note-plato.stanford.edu-1)

For example, if a philosophical zombie were poked with a sharp object, it would not feel any pain, but it would react exactly the way any conscious human would. Philosophical zombie arguments are used against forms of [physicalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicalism "Physicalism") and in defense of the [hard problem of consciousness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_problem_of_consciousness "Hard problem of consciousness"), which is the problem of accounting in physical terms for subjective, intrinsic, first-person, [what-it's-like-ness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What_is_it_like_to_be_a_bat%3F "What is it like to be a bat?") experiences. Proponents of philosophical zombie arguments, such as the philosopher [David Chalmers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Chalmers "David Chalmers"), argue that since a philosophical zombie is by definition physically identical to a conscious person, even its [logical possibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_possibility "Logical possibility") refutes physicalism. This is because it establishes the existence of conscious experience as a [further fact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Further_facts "Further facts").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_zombie#cite_note-Chalmers,_D._1996-2) Philosopher [Daniel Stoljar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Stoljar "Daniel Stoljar") points out that zombies need not be utterly without subjective states, and that even a subtle psychological difference between two physically identical people, such as how coffee tastes to them, is enough to refute physicalism.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_zombie#cite_note-3) Such arguments have been criticized by many philosophers. Some physicalists, such as [Daniel Dennett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Dennett "Daniel Dennett"), argue that philosophical zombies are logically incoherent and thus impossible, or that all humans are philosophical zombies;[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_zombie#cite_note-Dennett1991-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_zombie#cite_note-Dennett1995-5) others, such as [Christopher Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_S._Hill "Christopher S. Hill"), argue that philosophical zombies are coherent but metaphysically impossible.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_zombie#cite_note-:5-6)
